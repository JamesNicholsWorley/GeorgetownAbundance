How the Model Works

A technical explanation of the DC Housing Feasibility Simulator — how it identifies development sites, computes what can be built, and tests whether a project would earn a market return.

The central question: Given every privately-owned parcel in Washington DC, which ones are (a) physically capable of supporting new apartment construction, and (b) financially viable for a developer to build at current market rents?

The model works in four stages. First, it assembles a complete picture of every DC parcel — its size, what's on it today, what zoning allows, and what rents the neighborhood commands. Second, it computes the maximum building that could legally be constructed on each parcel. Third, it filters to "soft sites" — parcels where the existing building is substantially smaller than what zoning allows, making redevelopment a realistic possibility. Fourth, it runs a pro-forma financial test for each soft site to determine whether a market-rate apartment building would earn a sufficient return.

This methodology is adapted from the Terner Center for Housing Innovation at UC Berkeley, modified for DC's unique regulatory environment — particularly the federal Height Act of 1910, which imposes building height limits that exist in no other major American city.

Three policy scenarios are tested: a baseline (current rules), the Lewis George 2026 mayoral platform, and the McDuffie 2026 platform. The model is designed to be empirical, not directional — it reports what each scenario would physically and financially enable, without presuming what outcome is desirable.

Step 1 — Assembling the Parcel Universe

merge.py joins the six source datasets and computes several derived fields.

Land Cost

The model needs an estimate of what a developer would pay to acquire each parcel. In an ideal world, this would come from recent arms-length sales. In practice, only about 27.5% of eligible parcels have post-2022 transaction data. The model uses a three-tier fallback:

  1. DC assessed land value (from ITSPE) — available for most parcels
  2. Neighborhood median assessed land value per SF (same use class) — fills gaps where assessment is zero
  3. Ward-level median, then a citywide default of $50/SF — last resort

Assessed values systematically underestimate market value in DC — particularly in rapidly appreciating neighborhoods — which means the model's land cost inputs are probably conservative. The RLV test (described below) is calibrated against assessed land value, which partially corrects for this.

Ownership Filter

Parcels owned by government, universities, hospitals, foreign embassies, or religious institutions are classified as public_or_nonprofit and excluded from the private market-rate analysis entirely. These parcels are not in soft_sites.json and are not displayed on the dashboard map. The Python pipeline runs a separate social housing pro-forma for public land under the Lewis George scenario, but that analysis is not currently surfaced in the dashboard. The classification uses a combination of DC's tax-exempt rate flag and an owner name string match against a list of institutional patterns.

Zone Normalization

DC's ZR16 zoning codes have sub-variants (e.g., NMU-7B, MU-5B, BF-2A) that aren't directly listed in the Zoning Development Standards. merge.py maps these to the closest canonical zone code for development standards lookup. For example, all NMU (Neighborhood Mixed-Use) variants map to their MU equivalents per ZR16 §300.

Metro Adjacency

Metro adjacency is computed at two stages in the pipeline, with different precision at each:

  • Python pipeline (merge.py): Uses Advisory Neighborhood Commission (ANC) centroids as a proxy — the finest geographic unit in the zoning data. ANCs whose centroid falls within 0.5 miles of any DC Metrorail station are flagged as metro-adjacent. This flag drives McDuffie's Height Act amendment in the envelope computation.
  • Dashboard export (export_dashboard_data.py): Recomputes metro adjacency using actual parcel centroids from the parcel shapefile and haversine distance to each station. This per-parcel calculation determines the zone eligibility tier (el) displayed in the dashboard and is more accurate than the ANC proxy. It is computed at export time rather than pipeline time because it requires the geometry file.

The result is that the dashboard's tier-1 (metro-adjacent) designation uses true coordinates, not ANC approximations. The pipeline's metro_adjacent flag (ANC-based) is an acceptable proxy for envelope computation — the 0.5-mile threshold is much larger than the ANC centroid error (~0.1 miles).

# Three-tier land cost fallback:

Step 2 — The Development Envelope

envelope.py answers: what is the maximum building that can legally be built on this parcel? The answer is determined by three constraints, and the binding (most restrictive) one wins.

Constraint 1: FAR — Floor Area Ratio

FAR is the ratio of total building floor space to the lot area. A FAR of 3.5 on a 10,000 SF lot allows up to 35,000 SF of total floor space — which might be a 5-story building covering most of the lot, or an 8-story building covering less of it. DC's mixed-use zones range from FAR 1.0 (MU-1, low-density) to 8.0+ (MU-7, high-density transit-oriented). Single-family R zones don't use FAR — they use lot coverage rules (what percentage of the lot the building footprint can cover) combined with a story limit.

Constraint 2: Zoning Height Limit

Each zone code also has a maximum building height in feet. For example, MU-3A allows up to 65 feet. This can be more restrictive than FAR on narrow lots — a tall, thin building might hit the FAR limit before the height limit, or hit the height limit before the FAR limit, depending on lot dimensions.

Constraint 3: The Height Act of 1910

This is DC's most distinctive constraint. A federal statute — not a city zoning rule — limits building heights across the entire District. The formula is based on the width of the street the building fronts on:

Commercial buildings: Height = street width + 20 ft, maximum 130 ft
Residential buildings: Height = street width − 10 ft, maximum 90 ft
Pennsylvania Avenue NW: Special 160 ft exception for certain frontages

A typical DC side street is 60–70 feet wide, so a commercial building there can be at most 90–110 feet tall (7–9 stories), and a residential building can be at most 50–60 feet (4–5 stories). This is why DC doesn't have the dense mid-rise corridors visible in similarly-sized American cities — the Height Act effectively caps DC's entire residential fabric at 4–5 stories regardless of what the zoning code allows.

The street widths come from Roadway_Block.csv. When no street width data is available, the model falls back to the statutory maximums (130 ft commercial, 90 ft residential).

height_act_limit() envelope.py 
def height_act_limit(street_width_ft, use_type, address=""):
    """Return Height Act allowable height in feet.

    Commercial: ROW width + 20 ft, max 130 ft.
    Residential: ROW width − 10 ft, max 90 ft.
    Pennsylvania Ave NW: 160 ft exception.
    """
    penn_ave = address and "PENNSYLVANIA AVE" in str(address).upper()

    if pd.isna(street_width_ft) or street_width_ft <= 0:
        # No data — fall back to statutory maximums
        if penn_ave:
            return 160.0
        return 130.0 if use_type == "commercial" else 90.0

    if use_type == "commercial":
        limit = min(street_width_ft + 20, 130.0)
    else:
        limit = min(max(street_width_ft - 10, 20.0), 90.0)

    if penn_ave:
        limit = max(limit, 160.0)

    return limit

# GFA: compare FAR-constrained vs. height-constrained
if zoning_far > 0:

Step 3 — Identifying Soft Sites

Not every parcel that could accommodate a larger building will be redeveloped. The soft site filter identifies parcels where the gap between current development and what's allowed is large enough to plausibly motivate redevelopment.

Definition: A parcel is a "soft site" if its existing building's floor area is less than 50% of the maximum floor area zoning allows. A parcel with a 2,000 SF building on a lot that could support a 10,000 SF building is a soft site; a parcel already built to 80% of its allowable density is not.

The 50% threshold follows NYC's Department of City Planning Reasonable Worst-Case Development Scenario methodology, adapted for DC. In practice, it means: a two-story building on a lot where zoning allows ten stories is a soft site; a building using 8 of those 10 allowed stories is not. A developer must demolish the existing structure and bear the cost and risk of new construction — that decision is only rational when the "upside" from building larger substantially outweighs the current income from the existing building. DC's FAR-based zoning differs meaningfully from NYC's, so this 50% cutoff should be understood as a screening filter rather than a precise threshold.

A second filter requires that the parcel be large enough to support at least 3 implied apartment units (at the average unit size). This excludes parcels that are technically underbuilt relative to their zoning allowance but are too small to be viable multifamily development candidates from an institutional lending perspective.

Physical soft site filter run.py 
# A parcel is a physical soft site if:
# 1. Privately owned, has a valid street address, non-condo lot
# 2. Existing GBA < 50% of maximum buildable GFA
# 3. Net rentable SF ≥ minimum for 3 implied units

min_nr_sf = min_units * avg_unit_sf / efficiency_ratio  # 3,659 SF

candidates["is_physical_soft_site"] = (
    (candidates["existing_gba"].fillna(0) <
     soft_threshold * candidates["max_buildable_gfa"].fillna(0))   # < 50%
    & (candidates["max_buildable_gfa"].fillna(0) > 0)
    & (candidates["net_rentable_sf"].fillna(0) >= min_nr_sf)
)

Step 4 — The Pro-Forma

proforma.py asks the fundamental development question: if a developer demolished the existing structure, obtained financing, built the maximum allowable building, and then sold it to an institutional investor, would they earn enough return to justify the risk? This is called a "pro-forma" — a financial model that projects whether a project would pencil (developer shorthand for "the numbers work").

One constraint that doesn't appear in the pro-forma sliders: rents are market-set. A developer cannot simply charge more per month to make a project work — the market rate for apartments in a given neighborhood is determined by what tenants are willing and able to pay. The pro-forma takes current market rents as given and asks whether the resulting income can support the cost of building.

The pro-forma has two sides — revenue and costs — and three tests that must all pass.

Revenue Side

Revenue is annual apartment rent income, adjusted for vacancies and the requirement to include some affordable units under DC's Inclusionary Zoning (IZ) program.

Market-rate rents: Assigned by neighborhood-ward submarket from calibrated rent tables. Ward 7 and 8 submarkets average $1.50–1.90/SF/month; Ward 2 (downtown/Georgetown) averages $3.93/SF/month; the citywide default is $3.65/SF/month for new Class A construction.

Inclusionary Zoning (IZ): Buildings with 10 or more units must reserve a share of units as affordable. In the current model:

  • Wood-frame Type V: 11% of units at 60% of Area Median Income (AMI)
  • Concrete/steel Type I: 8.3% of units at 60% AMI
  • DC FY2026 four-person AMI: $163,900 → 60% AMI = $98,340/year household income
  • IZ unit rent (for a typical 1,000 SF unit): approximately $2,150/month

Note that in low-rent submarkets like Wards 7 and 8, the IZ rent ($2,150/month) can actually exceed the market rent for that neighborhood — affordable units are "more expensive" than market units. This is not a modeling error; it reflects that DC's AMI is anchored to the entire region's income (including suburban Maryland and Virginia), not just DC neighborhoods east of the Anacostia.

Vacancy: 6% (standard institutional underwriting assumption).

Operating expenses: $8,500 per unit per year (management, maintenance, insurance, reserves — from IREM/NAA DC metro benchmarks).

The Three Feasibility Tests

A parcel is "feasible" only if it passes all three tests simultaneously:

YoC
Yield-on-Cost ≥ 6.5%
YoC = NOI ÷ Total Development Cost
Annual rent income divided by total development cost. The developer needs to earn at least 6.5% — the 5.0% that investors pay for a finished building (the "cap rate," or going-in capitalization rate), plus a 1.5% premium for bearing construction risk. Think of it like a savings account: if a finished apartment building pays you 5% annually on your money, you'd only take the trouble to build one yourself if doing so would pay at least 6.5%. The extra 1.5% is the reward for bearing two or more years of construction risk, cost overruns, and market uncertainty.
RoC
Return on Cost ≥ 6.5%
RoC = (Stabilized Value ÷ TDC) − 1
The stabilized building value (NOI ÷ going-in cap rate) must exceed total development cost by at least 6.5%. This is a close cousin of YoC but measures the profit margin on cost in appreciation terms rather than income yield.
RLV
Residual Land Value ≥ Assessed Land Value
RLV = Stabilized Value − (TDC − Land Cost)
After accounting for all construction costs, the residual value the developer could "afford" to pay for the land must be at least as much as the current owner expects to receive (proxied by assessed land value). If not, the current owner won't sell at a price that makes the project work — the land is worth more as-is than as a development site. Technically: RLV is the amount left over after subtracting all non-land costs from the stabilized building value. This is the most commonly binding constraint in supply-constrained urban markets like DC.

The Rent-to-Pencil Metric

For infeasible parcels, the model computes a "rent-to-pencil" figure: the minimum monthly rent per square foot at which the project would just clear the YoC threshold, holding all other inputs constant. This is the market rent a developer would need to observe before building pencils — and quantifies the gap between current rents and the rent level required to unlock new construction.

feasibility tests + rent_to_pencil proforma.py 
# ── Three return tests ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
yoc  = noi / tdc
roc  = stabilized_value / tdc - 1
rlv  = stabilized_value - (tdc - land_cost)

yoc_ok = yoc  >= exit_cap + yoc_spread   # must beat 5.0% + 1.5% = 6.5%
roc_ok = roc  >= min_return_on_cost      # must be >= 6.5%
rlv_ok = rlv  >= assessed_land_value     # must cover what owner expects

feasible = yoc_ok and roc_ok and rlv_ok

# ── Rent-to-pencil: solve backward for the rent that clears YoC ────────
# Algebra: target_NOI = TDC × (exit_cap + spread)
# NOI = [(mkt_units × unit_sf × rent* + affrd_units × unit_sf × iz_rent)
#         × 12 × (1-vacancy)] - opex_total
# Solve for rent*:
target_noi = tdc * (exit_cap + yoc_spread)
opex_total = max_units * opex_per_unit_annual

rent_to_pencil = (
    (target_noi + opex_total) / ((1 - vacancy) * 12)
    - affordable_units * unit_sf * iz_rent
) / (market_units * unit_sf)

Social Housing Track (Lewis George — Pipeline Only)

Dashboard status: The social housing pro-forma is implemented in the Python pipeline (proforma.py) but is not currently shown in the dashboard. soft_sites.json contains only privately-owned parcels — public land is excluded at export time. The dashboard's JavaScript pro-forma engine models private market-rate development only.

Under the Lewis George scenario, the pipeline runs a parallel social housing pro-forma for publicly-owned parcels with fundamentally different economics:

  • Land cost = $0 (public land conveyed to the housing entity)
  • Required yield = 4.5% (cost of tax-exempt bond financing, vs. 6.5% for private)
  • Blended affordable rent across AMI tiers (30%/50%/80%/120% AMI mix)
  • No IZ set-aside (all units are affordable by definition)

The $0 land cost and 4.5% yield requirement substantially change the math — many sites that are infeasible for private development become viable for social housing. This analysis exists in the pipeline output parquets but would require a separate dashboard layer to display.

The Core Finding: The Rent Gap

Across all 4,250 soft sites under all three scenarios, zero private-market parcels pass the feasibility tests. That is not a modeling assumption — it is what the construction-cost and rent math produces when current market conditions are held constant. The model does not presume development is impossible; it quantifies how far the current market is from feasibility.

The fundamental problem is that the rent-to-pencil threshold (the rent required to make construction financially viable) is roughly 2–3× higher than actual market rents in the neighborhoods where the most developable land sits.

Ward 2 gets closest but still fails — primarily because the Height Act limits buildings to 6–8 stories on most DC streets, spreading high construction costs across fewer units than comparable cities.

Why is Ward 2 still infeasible despite the highest rents? The Height Act. On a typical 60-foot-wide DC street, the Height Act limits commercial buildings to 80 feet (about 6 stories). A 6-story building on a mid-sized lot doesn't generate enough units to spread the land cost, construction cost, and fee burden across. In Philadelphia or Chicago, the same lot at 15 stories generates 2.5× as many units to absorb those fixed costs.

What Would It Take to Make Development Pencil?

Because the model quantifies the gap rather than just labeling sites infeasible, it can answer this question directly. For a typical DC soft site to pass the YoC threshold, roughly one of the following changes would need to occur in isolation — or some combination in concert:

Required changes to unlock feasibility (approximate, per typical soft site):

• Rents rise 2–3× from current levels (e.g., Ward 7 from $1.60 to $4–5/SF) — unlikely without major regional income growth or severe supply restriction
• Construction costs fall ~30–40% — would require materials deflation and productivity gains well beyond current trends
• Land values drop 50–70% — possible only through economic distress, city land write-downs, or upzoning-induced reassessment
• Subsidies bridge the gap — e.g., a city land write-down program, tax abatements, or low-income housing tax credits covering 30–50% of development cost
• Height Act relief of 50%+ (to 130–180 ft across the city) — would double or triple unit counts on the same land, distributing fixed costs over more apartments

The dashboard's sliders allow direct sensitivity testing of these levers. No single adjustment in the plausible range produces feasibility; combinations (e.g., land write-downs + rent growth + Height Act reform) can close the gap in higher-rent submarkets.

Known Limitations

Every model involves simplifications. The following are the most material limitations of this analysis:

  • Front setbacks not modeled. The Zoning Development Standards dataset provides no front setback data. The buildable footprint is modestly overstated for parcels with front setback requirements.
  • Single efficiency ratio (82%). The GFA-to-net-rentable conversion should vary by building type: approximately 88% for Type V wood-frame, 82% for Type I mid-rise, 76% for Type I high-rise. Using a uniform 82% slightly overstates unit counts for Type V (favorable) and understates them for Type I high-rise (unfavorable). This is not material to the binary feasibility finding.
  • DC Water SAF approximation. The System Availability Fee is actually based on meter size, not unit count. The per-unit equivalent varies significantly by project scale. The $14,500/unit figure is a simplified average from DC Water's fee schedule. The slider in the dashboard allows sensitivity testing.
  • Land cost mostly from assessed values. Only 27.5% of eligible parcels have post-2022 sales data. Assessed values are generally lower than market values in DC, particularly in rapidly appreciating neighborhoods. This means the RLV test threshold (assessed land value) may be set too low, making parcels appear closer to feasibility than they are.
  • Parking costs not explicitly modeled. The model doesn't separately model parking construction. Lewis George's elimination of parking minimums is flagged in the scenario but has no direct cost effect because parking wasn't separately estimated.
  • No property tax modeling. DC PILOT (Payment in Lieu of Taxes) programs reduce operating costs for affordable projects. Not currently modeled.
  • Social housing not in dashboard. The Python pipeline includes a full social housing pro-forma for publicly-owned parcels under the Lewis George scenario, but soft_sites.json is limited to privately-owned parcels. Public land parcels are excluded from the map and from all dashboard statistics. Displaying social housing would require a separate JSON export and a parallel JavaScript pro-forma track.
  • Static rent assumption. The model uses current rents and does not forecast rent appreciation. A developer who believes rents will rise 20% over 5 years might build today even if the model shows infeasibility at current rents.
  • R-zone envelope approximation. R-zone parcels use lot coverage and story limits rather than FAR. The model approximates their envelopes using lot coverage × floor-to-floor height. More precise modeling would require parcel-specific surveys.
  • Construction costs are conservative (low). The model's hard cost figures ($280/SF Type V, $350/SF mid-rise, $420/SF high-rise) are at the lower end of what DC-area practitioners report in 2025–2026. Current estimates from industry sources run $375–$425/SF for podium mid-rise and $450–$500/SF for high-rise, reflecting ongoing materials cost inflation. If costs are understated by 10–15%, the rent gap widens further — making the zero-feasibility finding more robust, not less.
  • Height Act residential formula approximation. The model uses max(street_width − 10, 20) as a floor for residential height calculations on very narrow streets. This is a reasonable interpretation but is not explicitly grounded in the statute's narrow-street provisions (DC Code § 6-601.05). This is unlikely to affect more than a small number of parcels on atypical streets.
  • Cap rate convention. Stabilized value uses the going-in cap rate (4.75%); the YoC threshold tests against exit cap + 150 bps spread (6.50%). This follows standard DC developer underwriting (CBRE Q1 2025). Because stabilized value is computed at the lower (more generous) cap, the return-on-cost test (≥6.5%) is structurally slacker than the YoC test under current calibration — YoC is the binding hurdle in practice. A reviewer who prefers a single-cap convention would tighten value modestly and still find zero feasibility.
  • Property tax treatment in OpEx. Operating expenses ($8,500/unit/yr) are reported on the IREM/NAA benchmark basis; some sub-benchmarks include real estate taxes and some do not. DC Class 2 multifamily property tax (~$0.85 per $100 of assessed value) runs roughly $3,000–4,500/unit/yr on stabilized assets. If the model's OpEx underweights property tax, NOI is overstated by 15–25% — which would only deepen the zero-feasibility finding, not reverse it. PILOT and IZ tax-exclusion programs are also not modeled.
  • Loan-to-cost basis. Construction debt is sized at 65% of (hard + soft) cost rather than 65% of TDC. This understates debt and the resulting carry interest by roughly 10–15%. Correcting this convention raises TDC modestly and makes feasibility marginally harder to clear.
  • Static pro-forma; no rent or cost trending. The pro-forma uses today's rents and today's costs. Industry underwriting typically trends rents 2–3%/yr and costs 3–4%/yr over a 24–30 month construction window. Trending would lift NOI at delivery by ~5–8% and raise TDC by ~7–10%, with offsetting effects. We use static inputs because trending assumptions are themselves contested; the dashboard's sliders allow direct sensitivity testing of rent and cost levels.
  • IZ rent simplification. Affordable rent is computed using a 2.5-person household (≈ 0.875 × 4-person AMI) at 30% of income. The DHCD IZ Maximum Rent Schedule actually sets per-unit rents based on bedroom count + 1 household members, with utility allowance deductions. The flat 2.5-person convention introduces a small per-unit rent error (under 5%), partially offset by the omitted utility-allowance deduction.
  • No backtest against actual DC completions. The model has not been retroactively run on 2018–2020 inputs to test whether it would have flagged the ~5,000 units delivered during 2022–2024 as borderline-feasible. Backtest is a known v2 enhancement. Current results should be read as a structural feasibility statement at 2026 inputs, not a calibrated forecast.
